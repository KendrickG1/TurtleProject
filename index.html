1. Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle (Lepidochelys kempii)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL (Critically Endangered)

The Kemp's Ridley is the smallest of the world's sea turtles and is currently classified as Critically Endangered. These turtles are recognizable by their distinct olive-green carapace. Juveniles and sub-adults rely heavily on the relatively shallow, nutrient-rich coastal waters of the northern Gulf of Mexico, including those off the coast of Alabama, for critical foraging habitat. They primarily feed on small crabs, mollusks, and other bottom-dwelling invertebrates. The species is famous for its synchronized, mass nesting event called "arribadas," which occurs mainly in Tamaulipas, Mexico.

Nesting, Movement, and Key Threats

Nesting Cycle: While mass nesting occurs elsewhere, individual nesting crawls are rare but documented on Alabama's Gulf shores from April to July. Females typically lay 2-3 clutches, each containing around 100 eggs. Movement: They are highly aquatic and migratory. Hatchlings spend their early lives drifting in mats of Sargassum seaweed. Threats: The primary threats include accidental capture in commercial fishing gear (bycatch, especially shrimp trawling), entanglement in marine debris, and oil spills (such as the 2010 Deepwater Horizon spill), which have had a devastating impact on the Gulf population's recovery efforts.

Tracking Data: Nesting Crawl

Look for alternating flipper marks (often obscured by sand) and a central line from the plastron drag. Tracks are essential for field tracking models.
Footprint Type: Alternating, indistinct flipper prints in sand.

2. Loggerhead Sea Turtle (Caretta caretta)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL (Threatened)

The Loggerhead is named for its massive head, which supports powerful jaw muscles capable of crushing the shells of prey like clams, conchs, and crabs. They are the most common nesting sea turtle species along Alabama's Gulf Coast, with beaches like those in the Bon Secour National Wildlife Refuge being crucial for reproductive success. Loggerheads occupy coastal waters, estuaries, and the open ocean across the globe, migrating long distances between feeding and nesting grounds. Adults can reach up to 40 inches in shell length and weigh hundreds of pounds.

Nesting, Movement, and Key Threats

Nesting Cycle: Nesting occurs mainly at night from May to late October. Females will emerge from the sea to lay several clutches of approximately 110 eggs each, burying them deep in the dry sand above the high tide line. Movement: They are strong, long-distance swimmers. On land, they exhibit a slow, alternating gait. Threats: Major threats include coastal development, which degrades nesting habitat; artificial beachfront lighting, which disorients hatchlings; and plastic pollution, which they often mistake for food. Conservation efforts in Alabama focus on monitoring nests and reducing light pollution.

Tracking Data: Unique Footprint

Crawl tracks are wide, distinctive, and often described as messy or wide-swath. The front flipper marks are deep, wide, and create asymmetrical, alternating impressions.
Footprint Type: Wide, deep, alternating flipper marks with central drag.

3. Gopher Tortoise (Gopherus polyphemus)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL (Threatened)

The Gopher Tortoise is a vital keystone species found only in the Coastal Plain region of Alabama, south of the Fall Line. They are famous for excavating extensive burrows, which can be up to 48 feet long and 10 feet deep. These burrows provide essential shelter for over 350 other animal species, including the federally endangered Eastern indigo snake and burrowing owls, protecting them from heat, cold, and fire. Their preferred habitat is the open, sunny understory of longleaf pine forests with well-drained, sandy soils. Gopher tortoises can live for 40 to 80 years.

Nesting, Movement, and Key Threats

Nesting Cycle: Nesting occurs in the summer. Eggs (usually 3-15) are laid in a nest dug in an open, sunny patch of sand, often right outside the main burrow entrance. Movement: They are strictly terrestrial and slow-moving, using powerful, shovel-like front limbs to dig. Threats: The primary threat is habitat loss due to urbanization, agriculture, and commercial forestry. Fire suppression is also a problem, as the tortoises need the open, sun-drenched habitat maintained by natural, frequent fires. They are also vulnerable to being killed by vehicles and are sometimes illegally collected as pets.

Tracking Data: Footprint

Tracks are critical for population monitoring (outside burrows). The front footprint is wide, shovel-shaped with five distinct claws. The rear footprint is more rounded, almost elephantine.
Footprint Type: Shovel-like front prints; round rear prints.

4. Flattened Musk Turtle (Sternotherus depressus)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL (Threatened)

This small, freshwater turtle is endemic to Alabama, meaning it is found nowhere else in the world. It is restricted to the upper tributaries of the Black Warrior River system. Its most unique feature is its extremely low, or flattened, shell, which is a perfect adaptation for wedging itself into crevices and under submerged rocks and boulders. This defense mechanism requires a very specific habitat: clear, highly-oxygenated, free-flowing water with little siltation. They are primarily carnivorous, feeding on snails, clams, and aquatic insects they find on the streambed.

Nesting, Movement, and Key Threats

Nesting Cycle: Females produce small clutches (typically 1-4 eggs) twice a year, from May to July. They excavate very shallow nests in high, sandy banks near the stream's edge. Movement: They are shy, highly aquatic bottom-dwellers, rarely leaving the water. They have small home ranges, typically foraging along 20-30 meters of streambed. Threats: Because of their limited range and strict habitat requirements, they are highly vulnerable to water quality issues. The biggest threats are siltation from logging and agriculture, coal mining runoff, and the creation of impoundments (dams) that convert fast-flowing streams into still, silty reservoirs.

5. Alabama Red-bellied Turtle (Pseudemys rubriventris)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL (Endangered)

The Alabama Red-bellied Turtle is a large, beautiful freshwater species and is recognized as the Official State Reptile of Alabama. It is endemic to the Mobile-Tensaw River Delta and Mobile Bay drainage area in Mobile and Baldwin counties. It is primarily herbivorous, feeding on aquatic vegetation, and plays an important ecological role as a seed disperser. They prefer slow-moving water bodies with dense aquatic vegetation and plenty of sun-exposed basking sites, such as logs and sandbars, and can tolerate slightly brackish water near the coast.

Nesting, Movement, and Key Threats

Nesting Cycle: Females nest from April to early August, often creating a unique nest called a "nest plug" to hide the clutch of 4-9 eggs. To prevent nest flooding in the low-lying delta, they often travel significant distances to high, dry areas like man-made levees and sandbars. Movement: They are almost entirely aquatic, with terrestrial movement limited to females seeking nest sites. Threats: The population is severely threatened by habitat degradation from dredging and spoil deposition. Other major issues include heavy nest predation from animals like raccoons (whose populations thrive near human development) and accidental deaths from boat traffic within the Delta's waterways.

Tracking Data: Nesting Crawl

The track is a four-footed, webbed print crawl, with a distinctive, continuous wavy drag line from the plastron/tail. Tracking is focused on finding this crawl from the water's edge to the nest.
Footprint Type: Webbed-foot impressions leading to nest, with a clear central drag.
