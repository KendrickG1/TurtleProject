Our Story

Dedicated to Biodiversity in the Heart of Dixie

Alabama holds one of the highest rates of aquatic biodiversity in North America, yet many of its endemic species are facing extinction. Our organization was founded by local scientists and conservationists to specifically address the unique threats facing the state's rare turtle populations, from pollution in the Black Warrior River to habitat loss along the Gulf Coast.

We collaborate with state agencies, universities, and private landowners to implement effective, science-based recovery plans, ensuring these ancient reptiles thrive for generations to come.

Protecting Alabama's Natural Heritage.

1. Alabama Red-bellied Turtle (Pseudemys alabamensis)

Endangered (State and Federal Protection)

Species Overview & Habitat

The Alabama Red-bellied Turtle, Alabama's state reptile, is a large freshwater basking turtle endemic to the Mobile-Tensaw River Delta. They are easily recognized by the red or orange coloration on the underside of their shell (plastron), and the cusps (notches) on their upper jaw. They rely on slow-moving waters with abundant aquatic vegetation for food and basking logs.

Key Threats and Conservation

The primary threat to this species is habitat destruction and nest predation. Extensive human development in the Mobile-Tensaw Delta degrades water quality and destroys nesting beaches. Furthermore, their nests are heavily preyed upon by raccoons and other predators. Conservation efforts focus on head-starting programs (raising hatchlings in captivity until they are large enough to avoid predation) and protecting critical nesting sites from development.

Conservation Action Needed

Report any sightings of nesting females or protected habitats being disturbed to the Alabama Wildlife and Freshwater Fisheries Division.
Priority: Nesting beach protection and habitat restoration.

2. Flattened Musk Turtle (Sternotherus depressus)

Threatened (State and Federal Protection)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL (Threatened)

This small, freshwater turtle is endemic to Alabama, meaning it is found nowhere else in the world. It is restricted to the upper tributaries of the Black Warrior River system. Its most unique feature is its extremely low, or flattened, shell, which is a perfect adaptation for wedging itself into crevices and under submerged rocks and boulders. This defense mechanism requires a very specific habitat: clear, highly-oxygenated, free-flowing water with little siltation. They are primarily carnivorous, feeding on snails, clams, and aquatic insects they find on the streambed.

Nesting, Movement, and Key Threats

Nesting Cycle: Females produce small clutches (typically 1-4 eggs) twice a year, from May to July. They excavate very shallow nests in high, sandy banks near the stream's edge. Movement: They are shy, highly aquatic bottom-dwellers, rarely leaving the water. They have small home ranges, typically foraging along 20-30 meters of streambed. Threats: Because of their limited range and strict habitat requirements, they are highly vulnerable to water quality issues. The biggest threats are siltation from logging and agriculture, coal mining runoff, and the creation of impoundments (dams) that convert fast-flowing streams into still, silty reservoirs.

Tracking Data: Aquatic Tracks

Terrestrial footprints are rare. Tracking models must focus on aquatic movements—e.g., micro-tracks or disturbed sediment on the streambed, or identifying the brief nesting crawl on sandy banks.
Footprint Type: Small, webbed-foot impressions in mud/silt; rarely on land.

3. Loggerhead Sea Turtle (Caretta caretta)

Endangered (Federal Protection)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL (Threatened)

The Loggerhead is named for its massive head, which supports powerful jaw muscles capable of crushing the shells of prey like clams, conchs, and crabs. They are the most common nesting sea turtle species along Alabama's Gulf Coast, with beaches like those in the Bon Secour National Wildlife Refuge being crucial for reproductive success. Loggerheads occupy coastal waters, estuaries, and the open ocean across the globe, migrating long distances between feeding and nesting grounds. Adults can reach up to 40 inches in shell length and weigh hundreds of pounds.

Nesting, Movement, and Key Threats

Nesting Cycle: Nesting occurs mainly at night from May to late October. Females will emerge from the sea to lay several clutches of approximately 110 eggs each, burying them deep in the dry sand above the high tide line. Movement: They are strong, long-distance swimmers. On land, they exhibit a slow, alternating gait. Threats: Major threats include coastal development, which degrades nesting habitat; artificial beachfront lighting, which disorients hatchlings; and plastic pollution, which they often mistake for food. Conservation efforts in Alabama focus on monitoring nests and reducing light pollution.

Tracking Data: Unique Footprint

Crawl tracks are wide, distinctive, and often described as messy or wide-swath. The front flipper marks are deep, wide, and create asymmetrical, alternating impressions.
Footprint Type: Wide, deep, alternating flipper marks with central drag.

4. Gopher Tortoise (Gopherus polyphemus)

Threatened (Federal Protection in Western Alabama)

Species Overview & Habitat

The Gopher Tortoise is the only tortoise native to the southeastern United States. Unlike the other species on this list, it is a terrestrial species, meaning it lives entirely on land. It is considered a **keystone species** because its deep burrows provide shelter for over 350 other species, including snakes, frogs, and mammals. It is found in dry, sandy habitats, primarily the Longleaf Pine ecosystem, across the lower third of Alabama.

Burrowing, Diet, and Key Threats

Burrowing: Using its shovel-like forelimbs, the Gopher Tortoise digs burrows up to 30 feet long and 10 feet deep, maintaining stable temperature and humidity. Diet: They are herbivores, primarily grazing on grasses, legumes, and fruits. Threats: Habitat loss due to development, fragmentation of the longleaf pine forest, and vehicle collisions are the main threats. They are federally protected in their western range (west of the Mobile River), which includes much of Alabama. Conservation efforts focus heavily on prescribed burning to maintain their habitat's open canopy.

Tracking Data: Burrow Signatures

Tracking focuses on identifying the characteristic half-moon shape of the burrow entrance, which is usually found in sandy soil and may have a mound of excavated dirt nearby.
Footprint Type: Broad, clawed footprints around burrow entrances; four toes often visible.

5. Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle (Lepidochelys kempii)

Critically Endangered (Federal Protection)

Species Overview & Habitat

The Kemp's Ridley is the smallest and arguably the rarest of all sea turtles, often venturing into Alabama's shallow, nutrient-rich coastal waters. They have a round, grey-green carapace (shell) and a short snout. They are unique for their synchronized mass nesting events called "arribadas," which historically occurred primarily in Mexico, though solitary nesting has been observed in Alabama. They are carnivorous, eating crabs, mollusks, and fish.

Key Threats and Conservation

Despite major conservation successes, the species remains critically endangered. Key threats include accidental capture (bycatch) in shrimp trawls, plastic pollution, and loss of nesting beaches in their primary range. In Alabama, conservation focuses on reducing offshore fishing impact and emergency response for stranded or injured turtles found in Gulf waters. Because they are so small, hatchlings are particularly vulnerable to predation and disorientation.

Action: Emergency Stranding Protocol

If you encounter a stranded, injured, or deceased sea turtle, do not touch it. Immediately contact the Alabama Sea Turtle Stranding and Salvage Network.
Priority: Reducing bycatch mortality and public reporting.
