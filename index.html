1. Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle (Lepidochelys kempii)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL

The smallest and most endangered sea turtle globally. They have an olive-green shell. Alabama's coastal waters are crucial foraging grounds for juveniles and adults. Nesting is rare but documented on Alabama's Gulf shores.

Nesting & Movement

Nesting: Primarily daylight nesting from April to July. They lay 2-3 clutches (approx. 100 eggs each). Movement: Highly aquatic and migratory. Hatchlings drift on Sargassum mats. Adults are strong swimmers, moving between foraging areas and distant nesting sites.

Tracking Data: Nesting Crawl

Look for alternating flipper marks (often obscured by sand) and a central line from the plastron drag. Tracks are essential for field tracking models.
Footprint Type: Alternating, indistinct flipper prints in sand.

2. Loggerhead Sea Turtle (Caretta caretta)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL

Named for its large head and strong jaws. It is the most common nesting sea turtle species along Alabamaâ€™s Gulf Coast. They inhabit shallow coastal waters and the open ocean.

Nesting & Movement

Nesting: Primarily night nesting from May to late October. They lay large clutches (average ~110 eggs) high up on the beach. Movement: Powerful, migratory, long-distance swimmers. On land, they crawl slowly using an alternating pattern of front and rear flippers.

Tracking Data: Unique Footprint

Crawl tracks are wide, distinctive, and often described as messy or wide-swath. The front flipper marks are deep, wide, and create asymmetrical, alternating impressions.
Footprint Type: Wide, deep, alternating flipper marks with central drag.

3. Gopher Tortoise (Gopherus polyphemus)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL

A keystone species, famous for digging extensive burrows that shelter hundreds of other animals. Found in the Coastal Plain of Alabama, preferring dry, well-drained, sandy soils (sandhills, longleaf pine forests).

Nesting & Movement

Nesting: Eggs are laid in the summer in nests dug in open, sunny patches of sand, often near the burrow entrance. Clutch size is small (3-15 eggs). Movement: Terrestrial and slow. Movement is a steady walk. Front limbs are powerful and shovel-like for digging, which defines their track.

Tracking Data: Footprint

Tracks are critical for population monitoring (outside burrows). The front footprint is wide, shovel-shaped with five distinct claws. The rear footprint is more rounded, almost elephantine.
Footprint Type: Shovel-like front prints; round rear prints.

4. Flattened Musk Turtle (Sternotherus depressus)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL

A small, freshwater turtle endemic to Alabama (found nowhere else). Restricted to the upper Black Warrior River system. Requires clear, free-flowing water, low silt, and submerged rocks/boulders where it uses its flattened shell to hide in crevices.

Nesting & Movement

Nesting: Two small clutches per year (May to July), typically only 1-4 eggs. Nests are shallow, dug into high sandy banks near the stream's edge. Movement: A shy, aquatic bottom-dweller. Adults are mainly nocturnal in summer and stick to small home ranges (20-30m) as they crawl along the streambed foraging for mollusks.

5. Alabama Red-bellied Turtle (Pseudemys rubriventris)

Species Overview & Habitat in AL

The official State Reptile of Alabama. A large, herbivorous, freshwater turtle. Restricted primarily to the Mobile-Tensaw River Delta (Mobile and Baldwin counties). Prefers shallow, vegetated backwaters and areas of slightly brackish water.

Nesting & Movement

Nesting: April to early August. Females must leave the water to lay eggs in open, dry, elevated sandy areas (like levee banks and sandbars) to prevent flooding. Clutch size is 4-9 eggs. Movement: Highly aquatic. Spends most time foraging or basking on logs. Terrestrial movement is limited almost exclusively to the female's nesting crawl.

Tracking Data: Nesting Crawl

The track is a four-footed, webbed print crawl, with a distinctive, continuous wavy drag line from the plastron/tail. Tracking is focused on finding this crawl from the water's edge to the nest.
Footprint Type: Webbed-foot impressions leading to nest, with a clear central drag.
